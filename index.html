Where we'd start — based on our conversations

The strategy across these tabs is built to stand up infrastructure for five specific challenges that surfaced in our conversations. This page is a starting point for how I'd begin approaching them in the role.

01
TA needs more structure and automation.

Manual tasks across sourcing, scheduling, and pipeline hygiene are eating capacity. The function needs documented process, an opinionated tooling stack, and the discipline to retire workflows that don't scale — before adding more headcount.

02
Prioritization is hard when opportunity is everywhere.

Business momentum means every leader needs hires now. Without a stack-ranked plan, TA fills whoever's loudest instead of what's highest-leverage. The 90-day plan starts with HM 1:1s and a CEO pressure test to lock in the priority order — then defends it.

03
Behind on plan because People capacity is thin.

Hiring velocity is constrained by how many people can actually run a closed-loop process. The team build below adds dedicated capacity by phase — leadership and surge first (Phase 1), senior recruiters second (Phase 2), compounding sourcing and brand third (Phase 3) — without overspending before the operating model can absorb it.

04
We're not showing up in the market the way we should.

Top-of-funnel quality and inbound share both reflect the brand candidates see before they ever talk to a recruiter. The employer brand work fixes the careers page, the JDs, and the candidate experience so we attract the right people on purpose — and repel the wrong ones before they enter the process.

05
Finance has no durable forecast for when heads fill.

Without a model that translates approvals into capacity, burn, and a defensible close date, Finance can't predict when revenue unlocks. The capacity planner closes that gap — same TTF model, same recruiter ramp curves, same data feeding both the talent forecast and the headcount budget.

A note on what's not in here. L&D platform decisions, a culture survey vendor pick, and similar program work. Those are downstream of the foundation, and adding programs the system can't yet support is how People functions lose credibility before they earn it. The 90-day plan focuses on listening before fixing, the team build on infrastructure before execution, and the brand work on consistency before reach.

03 — Org & team build

Head of People at the top. Two directors, clean ownership lanes.

The org chart, the phased headcount, and the incentive structure are one decision, not three. The shape below is intentional — an operator at the top, distinct accountable lanes for pre-Day-1 (Talent) and post-Day-1 (HR Ops), and time-boxed surge capacity to hit the EOY number without the function carrying that volume permanently.

Operating philosophy

Most early People functions get built around traditional HR leadership — heavily policy-oriented, generalist, and reactive. This plan inverts that on purpose. The Head of People here is an operator, not a culture ambassador or policy owner. Their job is to embed with functional leaders, understand how each part of Ivo works, surface what's slowing teams down, and direct the People team to build the systems, processes, and programs that remove those constraints.

The bar is the brand. Every compromise on a hire is a brand event — not just an HR event. It signals to the existing team what the real standard is, regardless of what the careers page says. Ivo grows from 80 to 180 in 2026; that's roughly 100 net-new hires, on top of any backfills. The function below exists to deliver that volume without diluting what people came for.

Ten FTE plus three RPO contractors. Total of thirteen seats across three phases, sized so leadership and infrastructure land before execution capacity. Ivo's growth from 80 to 180 in 2026 puts the bulk of the hiring volume in the back half of the year — Phase 1 hires below are non-negotiable for the EOY plan; Phase 2 and 3 are the difference between scaling cleanly and scaling expensively.

Design principles
Why this shape, not a different one.
▼

Head of People as operator, not policy owner. Most early People functions get built around traditional HR leadership — heavily policy-oriented, generalist, reactive. This plan inverts that. The Head of People here is an operator and systems builder. They embed with functional leaders, surface what's slowing teams down, and direct the team to build the systems that remove those constraints. Not a culture ambassador or a benefits broker.

Two directors, clean ownership lanes. HR Operations owns everything after someone signs an offer — onboarding, HRIS, benefits, performance, offboarding. The Director of Talent owns everything before Day 1 — brand, sourcing, recruiting, candidate experience. This separation prevents the overlap and confusion that plagues early People teams when "talent" and "people" share scope.

Flat by design through the EOY number. Adding layers before they're necessary is the most expensive mistake a People function can make. The Head of People has two direct reports; the Director of Talent has seven. The shape revisits at ~220 employees — until then, additional hierarchy buys structure without buying output.

RPO embedded, not contracted. The three RPO recruiters operate inside the ATS, attend team syncs, and report to the Director of Talent for day-to-day work. They're held to the same performance standards as internal recruiters, with weekly pipeline reviews on the same cadence. Contract includes a wind-down clause at goal attainment — they sunset when the job is done, not on a calendar.

Capacity ratios & trigger points
When the ratios break, what to look for before they do.
▼

Recruiter capacity per recruiter. The Sr. Tech and Sr. Business Recruiters each carry 12–14 active reqs at any given time. Above that, time-in-stage starts creeping past five days at every step and offer accept rates drift down by 5–10 points without a clean comp explanation. Early warning: time-to-fill rising past 45 days on tiers where the historical average is below that; recruiters stop doing the small follow-ups that move candidates forward.

Sourcer to recruiter ratio. Roughly 1 sourcer per 1 senior recruiter at our role mix. The Technical Sourcer pairs with the Sr. Tech Recruiter; the Non-Technical Sourcer pairs with the Sr. Business Recruiter. If sourcing is shipping fewer than 8–10 qualified passive candidates per recruiter per week by Phase 3, the binding constraint is messaging or channel mix — adding sourcer headcount won't fix it.

HR Operations. One Head of HR Ops covers the post-Day-1 lifecycle through ~100 employees. Past that, the role needs a backstop — typically an HR Generalist or HR Ops Specialist — to handle ticket triage, onboarding execution, and vendor coordination. Trigger: company headcount hits 130, payroll error rate climbs above 0.5%, or onboarding NPS drops below 70.

RPO sunset. The three RPO contractors are budgeted for six months. Trigger to wind down: EOY hire goal hit, or sustained internal capacity above demand for two consecutive months. The contract includes a 30-day wind-down clause; we don't carry RPO into the next year unless the hiring plan demands it.

Sequencing logic
Why this order, not a different one.
▼

Phase 1 lands leadership and surge capacity simultaneously. The Head of People sets direction, the Head of HR Ops takes the back-office load off the company, and the Director of Talent stands up the function that the three embedded RPO contractors plug into on day one. The RPO contingent is not a "let's see if we need it" decision — it's the only realistic path to absorbing H2 hiring volume while internal recruiters are still being hired.

Phase 2 is the senior IC layer. Two full-cycle recruiters and the Talent Ops Specialist who keeps them functional. The point of Phase 2 is to start replacing RPO output with internal capacity — not because RPO is bad, but because $4k–$6k per hire in fees is meaningful spend on recurring volume.

Phase 3 is the compounding layer: sourcing, employer brand execution, and the junior generalist recruiter. These roles compound over six to twelve months, not three, so they're sequenced for when the operating model can absorb them. Adding them earlier produces less output than later, because there's no functional system for them to plug into yet.

If budget compresses
Specific trade-offs, not vague flexibility.
▼

If we lose the Jr. Generalist Recruiter: the senior recruiters absorb screening volume and the coordination load increases marginally. Acceptable. Reopen the role in Q1 of the following year if attrition or growth accelerates.

If we lose one of the sourcers: keep the Technical Sourcer; non-tech sourcing can run on RPO + Sr. Business Recruiter through year-end.

If we lose the Jr. Employer Brand role: the strategy still exists (Director of Talent owns it), but execution stalls. Recoverable in two quarters but the brand drag shows up in offer accept rates first.

If we lose Talent Ops & Coord: the senior recruiters get pulled into scheduling and ATS hygiene. This is the role I'd fight to keep above almost any other Phase 2/3 hire.

What I won't accept cutting: any of Phase 1, including the RPO contingent. The math on the EOY number doesn't work without that surge capacity.

Plan philosophy & trigger
Why a shared pool, why the binary 100-hire trigger.
▼

Shared pool, not individual commission. Every recruiter has a stake in every other recruiter's success. The team wins together or not at all. Individual commission designs reward closing your own reqs at the expense of helping a teammate close a harder one — a team pool inverts that incentive.

Binary trigger: 100 hires or no payout. Partial achievement does not unlock anything. This is unusual but intentional — it keeps the December close from becoming a panic, because the team knows the line is the line and acts accordingly all year. A "started" definition (offer signed and candidate started) means the trigger reflects real outcomes, not December offer-letter-stuffing.

One-time and non-recurring. Does not establish a comp precedent. Won't be referenced in offer letters, employment agreements, or future reviews. Leadership retains full discretion over whether to run a similar program in any future period.

Sourcer structure (separate pool)
Per-hire flat bonus by tier, multiplied by the same individual quality score.
▼

Sourcers are not part of the recruiter shared pool. They earn a per-hire flat bonus that compounds with their individual quality multiplier. A hire is "sourcer-originated" only if the candidate first entered the pipeline through direct sourcer outreach (LinkedIn InMail, cold email, outbound campaign) and is documented in the ATS at pipeline entry. Guardrails & non-precedent language
Forfeiture, gaming protection, the company's reservation of rights.
▼

Active employment required at payout. Eligible participants must be active employees on January 31, 2026. Voluntary resignations forfeit unpaid bonus allocation.

Hires rescinded by the company are excluded with no deduction. If a budget freeze or position elimination cancels an accepted offer, the recruiter is not penalized.

Modification clause. The company reserves the right to modify or cancel the plan with 30 days' written notice if material business conditions change (hiring freeze, restructure). Gaming, manipulation, or misrepresentation forfeits the entire bonus.

Final payout is discretionary, subject to CFO and CHRO approval, with written confirmation issued no later than January 15. The plan is confidential and not for distribution outside TA, HR leadership, and Finance.

04 — Headcount capacity planning

Every number is calculated. Every projection shows its reasoning.

A living headcount plan, not a static deck. The system models recruiting team capacity, forecasts when open roles will close, and surfaces risk before it becomes a miss. Below is how it works — what gets calculated, what's configurable, and how the numbers ladder up to a Finance-grade forecast.

Built for a high-bar, fast-scaling organization where roles are complex, loops are long, and the gap between pipeline volume and actual hires is large. Every model assumption accounts for that reality — not generic industry averages. Every projection includes an expandable "how was this calculated?" trace; every default has a cited basis; every anomaly flag explains its threshold.

The two numbers that matter

TTF and TTH are defined once, displayed everywhere, and never confused. Most miscommunication between Talent and Finance starts with these two numbers being treated as one.

Recruiter accountability

TTF — Time to Fill

Role Approval date → Offer Accepted / Committed

The recruiter's performance window. Closes the moment the offer is signed. This is the number recruiters are measured on.

Owner: Recruiting
Onboarding lag

TTH — Time to Hire

Offer Signature date → Employee Start Date

The gap between offer signed and the person actually showing up on payroll. Often 4–10 weeks for senior hires. Outside recruiter control but critical for Finance.

Owner: HR / Hiring Manager
Why this matters for Finance — TTF tells you when a recruiter closes a role. TTH tells you when the person actually shows up on payroll. Many Finance teams record headcount at Committed (offer signed); the model supports that explicitly. Roles where the high-end TTF puts them past December 31 are flagged "likely to miss EOY" and excluded from this year's headcount plan.

The TTF projection — eight inputs, applied in sequence
Not a guess. A model with eight configurable inputs. Output is always a range.
▼

Every projected time-to-fill range is built from the following factors. The base TTF for an IC3/Senior role is ten weeks; every input adjusts that baseline up or down.

InputWhat it does
Seniority multiplierIC3/Senior = 1.0× baseline. Staff = 1.3×. Principal = 1.6×. C-Level = 2.5×. Reflects narrowing talent pool and longer eval loops.
Priority levelP0 (critical) gets recruiter priority allocation, reducing projected TTF. P3 adds buffer.
Source channelReferral = −20% vs. baseline. Applied/Inbound = +10%. Agency = −15% speed (cost flagged separately).
TAM scoreIf the agent returns a TAM score below 40 (tight market), the projection extends. Above 60 = normal.
Specialization matchIf the assigned recruiter is outside their specialization, +15% is added (configurable in Settings).
Agency supportIf agency is engaged on the role, −15% adjustment. Cost flagged separately.
Backfill vs. net-newBackfills assume faster JD readiness. Net-new roles may require additional scoping time.
Historical data qualityIf ATS data is imported, actual TTF for this role type is weighted by data quality score (0–100).
The output is always three numbers. Optimistic — everything goes right: fast sourcing, strong pipeline, quick panel, first offer accepted. Expected — the most likely outcome based on all inputs; use this for Finance forecasting. Risk-adjusted — interviewer delays, offer declined, market tightness; use this for EOY risk assessment.
Recruiter capacity — from first principles
Not assumed to be 52 full working weeks. Calculated.
▼

Each recruiter's available weeks come out of a transparent formula. Defaults are configurable; OOO blocks are entered per recruiter.

Capacity formula
Gross weeks (52) − Holiday weeks (default 2.5) − PTO weeks (default 3.6, from 18 days) − Entered OOO blocks = Net available weeks

Ramp adjustment applied on top for recruiters hired within the last four months. SHRM benchmark for unlimited PTO at startups; ~11 US federal holidays for the holiday default.

Ramp schedule

New recruiters follow a fixed curve. The system applies it automatically based on start date.

Month 1
0%
No closes expected. Ramping into the role, team, and tools.
Month 2
40%
Early pipeline work. Some closes possible, no full req load yet.
Month 3
80%
Approaching full performance. Active req load near normal.
Month 4+
100%
Full capacity. Projections adjust automatically when this date is reached.

Sourcer impact. When a Tech Sourcer (Engineering roles) or GTM Sourcer (Sales roles) is supporting a recruiter, the system applies a +20% effective capacity boost on pipeline-dependent tasks for those roles. Configurable in Settings.

Interview panel capacity. Every interviewer is tracked across all roles they're on. Default overload threshold: 3 concurrent loops. When exceeded, +3 days per additional loop is added to the projected TTF for affected roles.

What makes a strong job description
Specific changes, not generic advice.
▼

Remove degree requirements unless legally required or the role genuinely needs the credential. Most don't. Degree gates suppress qualified applicant volume by 30–40% with no measurable downstream signal on hire quality.

Add a "what this role won't be" section. Setting non-expectations is more honest than over-promising and saves the candidate four hours of self-disqualification later.

Lead with the first 90 days. Most JDs lead with company boilerplate; that's the part candidates skim. The first 200 words should be what the person will actually be doing in their first quarter.

Include the comp range. Legally required in many states. Do it everywhere — the ones who care will appreciate it, and the ones who'd ghost you over the number self-select out before the screen.

A/B test two versions per JD for the first 60 days. Track apply rate, qualified-applicant rate, and decline-after-application rate. Pick the winner. Repeat.

Hiring manager voice, not corporate voice. The JD should sound like the person they'll work for, not like the website footer.

Length: 350–500 words. Most JDs are 1200+. Senior-candidate apply rate falls off a cliff past 600 words.

06 — How to work with me

A working manifesto, not a job description. Direct, honest, fast.

This is the document I send to direct reports the day they start. It says how I lead, how I think about AI in our work, what I expect from the team, and what I'll do for them in return. I share it up front so we don't have to figure it out as we go.

Operating beliefs
On AI in our work

AI handles the admin. We do what only people can do.

AI exists to take the work that eats time and produces nothing the candidate or hiring manager actually values — scheduling, pipeline hygiene, first-pass research, draft messaging, status updates. That's where automation belongs. What it leaves room for is the work no AI can do well: spending real time with candidates, giving them an experience that makes them want to join, advising hiring managers with context, and partnering with leadership to shape the priorities. The team's leverage comes from doing more of that, not less.

On the team

We're advisors, not order-takers. The bar is the brand.

Every recruiter on this team is a partner to the hiring team, not a service desk. When you think a hire is bad, push back. Bring the data, frame the tradeoff, and have the conversation. Our job is to ensure the hiring team understands the context, sees the tradeoff clearly, and is never sacrificing the company's bar to fill a seat. A "good enough" hire today is people debt for years. We're not in the business of accumulating that debt — we're in the business of preventing it.

On my role as your manager

Unblock, align, defend, coach.

My job is to make it easier for you to do yours. That means building the systems and structures that take friction out of the day-to-day, working alongside leadership to make sure priorities are clear and aligned with business goals, and standing in front of you when something needs defending. When a candidate you wanted ghosts, when messaging falls flat, when a hiring manager pushes back on a bar call — that's where I come in. To coach, to unblock, and to insert myself when it's the right move. You won't get hands-off management from me. You'll get someone who knows your roles, knows your candidates, and shows up when it matters.

The manual

The document I send to direct reports on Day 1.

Below is the version I share. It captures how I lead, how I communicate, and what I value. I want our working relationship clear from the start so we can spend the time on the work that matters.

Introduction

Welcome to the team. I understand that change can be intimidating, especially when you're adjusting to a new manager. I'm navigating this transition too, and I want to make sure we start off on the right foot. This manual outlines my approach to working together, what I value in our teamwork, and how we can grow and succeed.

Team guidelines
  1. Don't struggle in silence. If you're facing challenges, don't let them fester — bring me in. If you've been working on something for more than 15 minutes and still haven't found a solution, let's work through it as a team.
  2. Over-communication is key. Share information proactively, even if it seems minor. It keeps everyone aligned and prevents surprises.
  3. Everyone has a voice. This team values diverse perspectives. Don't be afraid to speak up — whether it's a suggestion, concern, or idea.
  4. Respectful disagreement is welcome. Healthy debate leads to better decisions. Disagree respectfully and stay open to other viewpoints.
  5. Don't be afraid to make mistakes. We learn by doing. Mistakes are opportunities to grow. Fail fast, fail forward.
  6. Bring your best self. If you're feeling off or overwhelmed, let me know. Your well-being is a priority — we'll work together to help you recharge.
  7. Celebrate success. Big or small, take the time to acknowledge wins as a team.
  8. Be accountable. We trust each other to do our best and hold ourselves accountable for our work. Integrity and transparency are essential.
  9. We trust each other. Our team thrives on honesty and mutual trust. Keep communication open, stay aligned.
  10. Take pride in your work. Focus on growing personally and take pride in everything you do. When each of us strives to do our best, we all succeed.
How I view success

For me, the success of our team is defined by three things.

Achievement

Success is reaching the goals we set for ourselves and the team — not just hitting targets but consistently delivering high-quality work that contributes to the bigger picture. When each of us focuses on doing our part, we contribute to the overall success of the company. That means taking ownership of our work and maintaining a high standard of excellence. We'll face challenges; staying focused, keeping a positive mindset, and trusting the process is how we get there. Once we hit a goal, I believe in setting a higher one. Success isn't static — it's a continuous journey of growth.

Improvement & curiosity

Success is also about growth — learning from mistakes, seeking opportunities to improve, and staying curious about how we can do better. We all make mistakes; what matters is how we learn from them. "Fail fast, succeed sooner": every setback is a chance to pivot and improve. The world around us, especially in tech and SaaS, is always evolving. Stay curious, keep learning, and we'll stay at the top of our game.

Engagement

I want everyone on the team to be excited to come in every day, to feel that the work they do is meaningful and contributes to their growth. Engagement means being present, mentally and emotionally. Communicate openly — whether you're excited about something new or need support when you're feeling stretched. If you're burned out, facing challenges, or simply need a break, talk to me. The work will still be here when you get back. Take care of your well-being first.

Communicate with me. If you need a mental health day, take it. If you're unhappy with something I did or said, let's talk it through. If something interests you and you want to explore it, tell me — I might not be able to make it happen right away, but I'll see what I can do.
