Operating philosophy

Most early People functions get built around traditional HR leadership — heavily policy-oriented, generalist, and reactive. This plan inverts that on purpose. The Head of People here is an operator, not a culture ambassador or policy owner. Their job is to embed with functional leaders, understand how each part of Ivo works, surface what's slowing teams down, and direct the People team to build the systems, processes, and programs that remove those constraints.

The bar is the brand. Every compromise on a hire is a brand event — not just an HR event. It signals to the existing team what the real standard is, regardless of what the careers page says. Ivo grows from 80 to 180 in 2026; that's roughly 100 net-new hires, on top of any backfills. The function below exists to deliver that volume without diluting what people came for.

A note on what's not in here. L&D platform decision, a culture survey vendor pick, ect. Those are downstream of the foundation, and adding programs the system can't yet support is how People functions lose credibility before they earn it. The 12-week plan focuses on listening before fixing, the team build on infrastructure before execution, and the brand work on consistency before reach.

Phase 1 · Weeks 1–2
Listen, diagnose & prioritize.
Understand the business before making a single decision. Click any week to see the execution.
▼

Every action gets filtered through what the business actually needs, not what TA assumes it needs. The output of Phase 1 is a stack-ranked role list pressure-tested with the CEO and a headcount proposal submitted for approval — not a finished plan, but enough signal to move on Week 3.

Week 1 — stakeholder listening tour
Stakeholder sessions, sourcing diagnostic, CEO pressure test.
▼
  • Stakeholder meetings with every functional leader. Same five questions: org vision now and EOY; top 3 team priorities; material business impact of each open role; where the team is performing well; where the gaps are creating drag.
  • After each session: source 5–8 diverse candidates against the prioritized roles. Diagnostic, not delivery — surface preference patterns, blind spots, what HMs respond to vs. what they say they want.
  • Alignment meeting with the CEO. Frame everything in time, cost, risk, velocity — every stakeholder you've spoken to is reading the same room from a different angle, and the CEO is the integrator.
  • Stack-rank open roles using three lenses: business priority, current team strain, future direction.
  • Take the prioritized stack to the CEO. Not for approval — for a pressure test of the logic and an alignment on talent philosophy.
  • Deliverables: stakeholder insights summary (one page per team), role prioritization stack rank, bias and preference profile by team, themes across sessions.
Week 2 — mapping & viability + headcount proposal
Translate priorities into a realistic execution plan. Submit the headcount proposal by Friday.
▼
  • Role mapping: geographic breakdown, department and seniority distribution, which roles need programs (university, events, sourcing campaigns).
  • Market viability deep dive: pull historical TTH from Ashby for comparable roles; benchmark goals against what's actually achievable. Flag unrealistic goals early — not in month five when it's too late to recover.
  • Tech stack audit: tool spend vs. ROI, where hires are actually coming from, where automation can reduce administrative burden.
  • Capacity planning model: based on projected role load, role type, and department complexity — how many TA team members are actually required.
  • Headcount proposal + org chart submitted for approval by end of Week 2. Grounded in the role map and capacity model, not assumptions. TA team reqs open Week 3.
  • Guiding principle: minimum viable operations first. Stability before optimization.
Phase 2 · Weeks 3–8
Build, fix & activate.
Active recruiting begins. Process gets observed, then improved. Brand work goes live.
▼

By Week 6 leadership has visibility every Monday morning. By Week 8 the brand strategy is live. Phase 2 is six weeks of operating-mode TA — running pipeline, fixing what's broken, and shipping the infrastructure that lets the function scale past one operator.

Week 3 — active recruiting + TA hiring opens
Reqs go live. Process observed, not changed yet.
▼
  • TA team reqs live. Active sourcing begins in parallel with open business roles. Target: offers extended weeks 5–6, starts week 7.
  • Process assessment: meet with the existing TA team to understand suspected bottlenecks; independently observe candidate flow end-to-end for both technical and GTM loops.
  • Light HM brief before HMs interview this week — what's expected, how to give structured feedback, SLA for turnaround. Not full enablement (that's Week 7), just table stakes.
  • Recruiter enablement v1 begins: process documentation, company pitch training, comp/equity training, hiring philosophy, do's and don'ts.
  • Deliverables: TA reqs live; process map for tech and GTM; friction log (observations, not proposals); recruiter enablement v1 underway.
Week 4 — sourcing intelligence + brand activation
Stop fishing in the wrong ponds. Make the company visible where target talent actually lives.
▼
  • Sourcing audit: what candidates are saying about us, where the talent we want lives, what we're getting vs. what we need.
  • Talent mapping: build sourcing bank by company, community, event. For high-priority roles, identify alum networks and online communities aligned with team direction.
  • For meetup groups: scrub the full attendee list, connect on LinkedIn, work toward attending or hosting an event within two weeks.
  • Launch LinkedIn posting cadence. A/B test outreach messages — subject framing, role positioning, mission vs. growth vs. comp angles.
  • Deliverables: sourcing intelligence brief, talent map, event activation plan (next 4 weeks), posting cadence launched, A/B variants live.
Week 5 — offer & pre-boarding audit
The space between offer signature and Day 1 is where companies lose people without knowing it.
▼
  • Deep-dive the offer extension workflow — what's the experience from verbal to signature?
  • Where can we automate and streamline without making it feel transactional?
  • Build pre-start engagement playbook v1: swag one week before start, invite to relevant team events, slow-drip welcome messages, structured check-ins from recruiter and HM.
  • Goal: by Day 1, new hires already feel like part of the team.
  • Deliverables: offer + pre-boarding gap analysis, pre-start engagement playbook v1, automation map, proposal for structural changes needing leadership sign-off.
Week 6 — dashboards, SHP tracker, first plan projection
Course correct early. Surface trajectory before it becomes a crisis.
▼
  • Monthly leadership hiring review cadence launched: metrics, tracking vs. plan, role adjustments, headcount changes. All decisions transcribed and followed up via email.
  • Ashby dashboards live for HMs (org-level), the exec team (company-wide), and CEO (high-level mapped to business goals).
  • SHP (Stakeholder Hiring Plan) Tracker built — prioritization and SLAs by priority, visible to TA, hiring teams, and HMs. Progress bars on documentation completion. Real-time visibility into where slowdowns sit.
  • First hiring plan projection: at current velocity, where do we land by EOY? Surface the gap in the first leadership review — not month 11.
  • TA team offers extended (target: 30–40% of function).
Week 7 — TA partner onboarding + HM enablement audit
Ramp new TA partners fast. Audit HM enablement before gaps become a bottleneck at scale.
▼
  • TA partner onboarding: Day 1 orientation, tools access, hiring philosophy. Days 2–3, meetings with HMs and stakeholders. End of week, actively sourcing on prioritized roles.
  • HM enablement audit: where does the current onboarding for HMs work, where do things slip? No HM should be unclear on what's expected of them or when.
  • Build HM master checklist — zero gaps first, optimization later.
  • SCALE framework applied to all process design: Signal through real work, Compounding teams, AI-enabled, Leverages judgment, Elevates the work and team.
  • Deliverables: TA onboarding executed, new partners sourcing, HM gap analysis, HM master checklist v1, proposal for HM improvements.
Week 8 — full employer brand strategy + ToF campaign
You can't outrecruit a weak brand. Build the strategy that changes what candidates see and hear before they ever talk to a recruiter.
▼
  • Brand audit: pull 4 weeks of lightweight data, build full strategy, present to the CEO and Marketing leadership.
  • Three brand signals: what you say (content), what you do (process and decisions), what others say about you (Glassdoor, Built In, third-party).
  • Five content pillars: product velocity, team growth and culture, milestones, leadership thought leadership, career growth stories.
  • What we test: outreach message framing, content format (video vs. static vs. carousel), posting cadence, mission vs. growth vs. culture angle.
  • Decision: continue lightweight or escalate to a full marketing campaign in Month 5 based on traction.
  • Deliverables: brand audit, content strategy, posting calendar (weeks 9–12), brand metrics baseline, Built In/Glassdoor activation plan.
Phase 3 · Weeks 9–12
Optimize & scale.
Tighten what six weeks of execution is telling us. Move from reactive to proactive talent engine.
▼

The team is hired and ramping. By Week 12 the function should be operationally documented, the hiring plan reality-checked, and the year-end roadmap built. The Week-12 readout to the CEO isn't a status report — it's the partnership document that closes the 90-day window and opens the next twelve months.

Week 9 — process optimization + comp audit + TA performance
Tighten process. Set the performance foundation for the team.
▼
  • Process champions debrief: bring back two champions (one tech, one non-tech) for structured feedback. What worked, what still has friction, what candidates are saying. Finalize process changes on real signal, not theory.
  • Comp benchmarking audit: pull offer accept rate and decline reasons from Ashby, cross-reference market data for top tech and GTM roles. Frame to the CEO and CFO as a revenue and retention conversation, not an HR ask.
  • Set individual TA KPIs: pipeline volume, screens, submittals/week, time-to-submit, accept rate. Establish 1:1 cadence — weekly operational, monthly coaching.
  • Build shared team dashboard — transparency creates accountability without micromanagement.
  • First funnel conversion review (tech vs. GTM separated). Identify top 2 conversion problems and build a fix for each.
Week 10 — programs assessment + referral launch
Move from reactive to a proactive talent engine. Prioritize programs by business need, readiness, and ROI timeline.
▼
  • Programs assessment delivered to the CEO with timeline benchmarking: Referral Program (now), Silver Medalist Re-engagement (Month 4–5), Diversity Sourcing (Month 5–6), University/Early Career (Month 6–9), Full Brand Campaign (Month 5–6 if traction confirmed).
  • Referral program launch: simple, high-trust structure, bonus tied to seniority, paid at 90-day retention milestone. Promote via Slack, all-hands, and 1:1 asks during team check-ins.
  • Silver medalist re-engagement cadence built — warm, vetted candidates who almost made it. Low cost, high close rate.
  • Brand traction check: pull 6-week brand metrics — InMail response, follower growth, post engagement, Glassdoor trend, applicant source quality. Decision point — continue lightweight or escalate.
Week 11 — hiring plan reality check + ToF deep dive
Map landing position by department. Surface gaps with levers, not opinions.
▼
  • Hiring plan reality check: hires made, roles in final stages, roles with strong pipelines, roles stalled. Project at current velocity by department — tech and GTM will land differently. If there's a gap, surface it with a clear plan and recommended levers.
  • Top-of-funnel deep dive: is volume improving? Track InMail response trends, apply rates, inbound vs. outbound week over week. If still low, diagnose whether issue is message, channel, role positioning, or comp.
  • HM accountability — data-led: pull SHP tracker data. Which hiring teams have completed documentation on time? Which create delays? Bring patterns to the exec team with data, not opinions.
  • TA team health check: are KPIs being hit, where does coaching need to happen, are partners blocked on something systemic, is workload distributed appropriately?
Week 12 — operational documentation + year-end roadmap
Close the 90-day window. Open the next twelve months with a forward-looking partnership document.
▼
  • Operational documentation audit: every process, playbook, workflow built over 12 weeks is documented, accessible, owned. No single points of failure — if anyone is out for two weeks, the team executes cleanly.
  • Year-end roadmap: month-by-month plan to hit the hiring plan. Account for seasonal slowdowns (holiday pipeline drag), offer decline risk on late-stage candidates, ramp time for hires made in months 4–5, programs coming online in months 5–6.
  • CEO alignment session: what worked, what still needs investment, what decisions need to happen in the next 30 days to protect year-end outcomes. Align priorities for months 4–6.
  • TA function retrospective (internal): what's working, what's creating friction, what does the team need to do their best work? Builds trust and surfaces problems early.

Design principles
Why this shape, not a different one.
▼

Head of People as operator, not policy owner. Most early People functions get built around traditional HR leadership — heavily policy-oriented, generalist, reactive. This plan inverts that. The Head of People here is an operator and systems builder. They embed with functional leaders, surface what's slowing teams down, and direct the team to build the systems that remove those constraints. Not a culture ambassador or a benefits broker.

Two directors, clean ownership lanes. HR Operations owns everything after someone signs an offer — onboarding, HRIS, benefits, performance, offboarding. The Director of Talent owns everything before Day 1 — brand, sourcing, recruiting, candidate experience. This separation prevents the overlap and confusion that plagues early People teams when "talent" and "people" share scope.

Flat by design through the EOY number. Adding layers before they're necessary is the most expensive mistake a People function can make. The Head of People has two direct reports; the Director of Talent has seven. The shape revisits at ~220 employees — until then, additional hierarchy buys structure without buying output.

RPO embedded, not contracted. The three RPO recruiters operate inside the ATS, attend team syncs, and report to the Director of Talent for day-to-day work. They're held to the same performance standards as internal recruiters, with weekly pipeline reviews on the same cadence. Contract includes a wind-down clause at goal attainment — they sunset when the job is done, not on a calendar.

Capacity ratios & trigger points
When the ratios break, what to look for before they do.
▼

Recruiter capacity per recruiter. The Sr. Tech and Sr. Business Recruiters each carry 12–14 active reqs at any given time. Above that, time-in-stage starts creeping past five days at every step and offer accept rates drift down by 5–10 points without a clean comp explanation. Early warning: time-to-fill rising past 45 days on tiers where the historical average is below that; recruiters stop doing the small follow-ups that move candidates forward.

Sourcer to recruiter ratio. Roughly 1 sourcer per 1 senior recruiter at our role mix. The Technical Sourcer pairs with the Sr. Tech Recruiter; the Non-Technical Sourcer pairs with the Sr. Business Recruiter. If sourcing is shipping fewer than 8–10 qualified passive candidates per recruiter per week by Phase 3, the binding constraint is messaging or channel mix — adding sourcer headcount won't fix it.

HR Operations. One Head of HR Ops covers the post-Day-1 lifecycle through ~100 employees. Past that, the role needs a backstop — typically an HR Generalist or HR Ops Specialist — to handle ticket triage, onboarding execution, and vendor coordination. Trigger: company headcount hits 130, payroll error rate climbs above 0.5%, or onboarding NPS drops below 70. Any one is a yellow flag.

RPO sunset. The three RPO contractors are budgeted for six months. Trigger to wind down: EOY hire goal hit, or sustained internal capacity above demand for two consecutive months. The contract includes a 30-day wind-down clause; we don't carry RPO into the next year unless the hiring plan demands it.

Sequencing logic
Why this order, not a different one.
▼

Phase 1 lands leadership and surge capacity simultaneously. The Head of People sets direction, the Head of HR Ops takes the back-office load off the company, and the Director of Talent stands up the function that the three embedded RPO contractors plug into on day one. The RPO contingent is not a "let's see if we need it" decision — it's the only realistic path to absorbing H2 hiring volume while internal recruiters are still being hired.

Phase 2 is the senior IC layer. Two full-cycle recruiters and the Talent Ops Specialist who keeps them functional. The point of Phase 2 is to start replacing RPO output with internal capacity — not because RPO is bad, but because $4k–$6k per hire in fees is meaningful spend on recurring volume.

Phase 3 is the compounding layer: sourcing, employer brand execution, and the junior generalist recruiter. These roles compound over six to twelve months, not three, so they're sequenced for when the operating model can absorb them. Adding them earlier produces less output than later, because there's no functional system for them to plug into yet.

If budget compresses
Specific trade-offs, not vague flexibility.
▼

If we lose the Jr. Generalist Recruiter: the senior recruiters absorb screening volume and the coordination load increases marginally. Acceptable. Reopen the role in Q1 of the following year if attrition or growth accelerates.

If we lose one of the sourcers: keep the Technical Sourcer; non-tech sourcing can run on RPO + Sr. Business Recruiter through year-end. Cost: a temporary CPH bump on GTM hires that recovers when sourcing comes back online.

If we lose the Jr. Employer Brand role: the strategy still exists (Director of Talent owns it), but execution stalls. LinkedIn cadence drops, Glassdoor ages out, content calendar becomes aspirational. Recoverable in two quarters but the brand drag shows up in offer accept rates first.

If we lose Talent Ops & Coord: the senior recruiters get pulled into scheduling and ATS hygiene, which is the worst use of their time at the worst possible moment. This is the role I'd fight to keep above almost any other Phase 2/3 hire.

What I won't accept cutting: any of Phase 1, including the RPO contingent. The math on the EOY number doesn't work without that surge capacity, and the leadership trio either lands together or the function can't be staged from anywhere.

Plan philosophy & trigger
Why a shared pool, why the binary 100-hire trigger.
▼

Shared pool, not individual commission. Every recruiter has a stake in every other recruiter's success. The team wins together or not at all. Individual commission designs reward closing your own reqs at the expense of helping a teammate close a harder one — a team pool inverts that incentive. It also reduces pressure to push borderline candidates over the line just to claim the close.

Binary trigger: 100 hires or no payout. Partial achievement does not unlock anything. Ninety-eight is not ninety-eight percent. This is unusual but intentional — it keeps the December close from becoming a panic, because the team knows the line is the line and acts accordingly all year. A "started" definition (offer signed and candidate started) means the trigger reflects real outcomes, not December offer-letter-stuffing.

One-time and non-recurring. Does not establish a comp precedent. Won't be referenced in offer letters, employment agreements, or future reviews. Leadership retains full discretion over whether to run a similar program in any future period.

Not all hires carry equal complexity. The point system reflects market difficulty.
▼

Senior tech roles demand deeper sourcing, longer cycles, and specialized evaluation. The weighting below reflects that — and crucially, all classifications are locked at req open. Retroactive changes to inflate point value are not permitted.

Total disbursement never exceeds the pool.

Sourcer structure (separate pool)
Per-hire flat bonus by tier, multiplied by the same individual quality score.
▼

Sourcers are not part of the recruiter shared pool. They earn a per-hire flat bonus that compounds with their individual quality multiplier. A hire is "sourcer-originated" only if the candidate first entered the pipeline through direct sourcer outreach (LinkedIn InMail, cold email, outbound campaign) and is documented in the ATS at pipeline entry. Referrals, inbound applicants, and re-engagement of previously declined candidates are not credited.

Guardrails & non-precedent language
Forfeiture, gaming protection, the company's reservation of rights.
▼

Active employment required at payout. Eligible participants must be active employees on January 31, 2026. Voluntary resignations forfeit unpaid bonus allocation. Standard practice; mentioned because some plans omit it and end up paying people who've already moved on.

Hires rescinded by the company are excluded with no deduction. If a budget freeze or position elimination cancels an accepted offer, the recruiter is not penalized — the deduction structure is for hires the recruiter could have screened out, not for company decisions.

Modification clause. The company reserves the right to modify or cancel the plan with 30 days' written notice if material business conditions change (hiring freeze, restructure). Gaming, manipulation, or misrepresentation of attribution, surveys, classifications, or pipeline records forfeits the entire bonus and may trigger disciplinary action.

Final payout is discretionary, subject to CFO and CHRO approval, with written confirmation issued no later than January 15. The plan is confidential and not for distribution outside TA, HR leadership, and Finance.

05 — Capacity planner for finance

Headcount approvals are abstract until somebody translates them into burn.

A working model that turns the headcount plan into the three numbers Finance actually needs: monthly burn, recruiter capacity, and a defensible close date. Below is the why, then how the inputs fit together, then the live prototype.

Finance cares about this model for four reasons. None of them are hypothetical.

  1. Runway translation. Headcount approvals don't show up in the cash forecast until somebody converts seats into a monthly number. The model does that conversion in one place, with the assumptions visible, so Finance isn't rebuilding it in a separate sheet.
  2. Hiring velocity risk. If Talent's stated capacity and Talent's actual capacity differ by ten hires, Finance is building a budget on the wrong assumption. Better to know in May than in October. The model surfaces the gap when it exists.
  3. People costs as percent of revenue. At what ARR milestone does the headcount plan stop being affordable? The model lets Finance and the Head of People have that conversation with numbers instead of feelings.
  4. Trade-off legibility. Delay a hire by a quarter, extend RPO, hire a contractor, open a role offshore — these are not values debates. They're number comparisons. Putting them in a shared model makes the comparison legible to everyone in the room.
Input layer 1 — headcount plan
Total approved headcount, broken down by department and quarter.
▼

What's in it. Approved headcount, segmented by function and target start quarter. Source-of-truth, not the wishlist. Anything that isn't in this layer doesn't exist for capacity purposes.

Why it matters. The number that gets argued about in a planning meeting and the number that actually got approved are often different by Q2. This layer is the version of the truth Finance signed off on, and it's the version the model assumes. If a hire is happening that isn't in here, it's a process question, not a model question.

Failure mode prevented. Hiring against verbal commitments that get walked back at the next budget review. If it's not in this layer, it's not protected.

Input layer 2 — velocity and capacity
Recruiter count, time-to-fill by tier, offer acceptance rate.
▼

What's in it. Recruiter headcount (FTE plus RPO), average time-to-fill segmented by role tier, and rolling-90-day offer acceptance rate. Three numbers, kept current by the recruiting ops cadence.

Why it matters. Capacity is a function of cycle time and conversion, not headcount alone. Two recruiters at a 35-day TTF outproduce four recruiters at a 70-day TTF. If the constraint is process, adding recruiter headcount doesn't help — it spreads the same broken process across more people.

Failure mode prevented. Adding recruiter headcount when the actual bottleneck is the comp band, the interview loop length, or the time-from-offer-to-accept. The model forces the diagnosis to happen before the spend.

Input layer 3 — loaded cost parameters
Average base, benefits loading, equity refresh assumptions.
▼

What's in it. Average base salary by tier, benefits and tax loading percentage, annual equity refresh rate. The numbers Finance already maintains for the operating plan, surfaced here so the headcount math uses the same source.

Why it matters. A hire is not a salary. It's a salary plus 25–32% in benefits, taxes, and equity dilution. Approving headcount on base alone is how Finance gets surprised in Q3 by a budget gap that wasn't in anyone's spreadsheet — because the spreadsheet was wrong by design.

Failure mode prevented. Decisions made on base comp alone, then re-litigated when the loaded cost shows up.

Ivo’s employer brand is built to consistently attract exceptional technical and SaaS talent by making the standard unmistakable at every touchpoint. What makes a strong job description
Specific changes, not generic advice.
▼

Remove degree requirements unless legally required or the role genuinely needs the credential. Most don't. The data on this is clean — degree gates suppress qualified applicant volume by 30–40% with no measurable downstream signal on hire quality.

Add a "what this role won't be" section. Setting non-expectations is more honest than over-promising and saves the candidate four hours of self-disqualification later.

Lead with the first 90 days. Most JDs lead with company boilerplate; that's the part candidates skim. The first 200 words should be what the person will actually be doing in their first quarter.

Include the comp range. Legally required in many states. Do it everywhere — the ones who care will appreciate it, and the ones who'd ghost you over the number self-select out before the screen.

A/B test two versions per JD for the first 60 days. Track apply rate, qualified-applicant rate, and decline-after-application rate. Pick the winner. Repeat. Most companies have never done this, which is why most JDs read identically.

Hiring manager voice, not corporate voice. The JD should sound like the person they'll work for, not like the website footer.

Length: 350–500 words. Most JDs are 1200+. Senior-candidate apply rate falls off a cliff past 600 words, and the data is consistent across functions.
